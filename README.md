### Project Overview
This project analyzes the correlation between lifestyle and mental health. I studied factors like stress, sleep, diet, exercise and social interaction to identify key trends and promote positive habit changes using SQL Server for analysis and Excel for visualization.

### Key Questions Explored

- How do lifestyle habits and mental health outcomes vary by country?
- How do gender differences interact with lifestyle habits and mental health?
- Which lifestyle factors show the strongest correlation with happiness levels?
- Are there identifiable clusters of people with similar lifestyle patterns?

### Key Findings
**1. Country-Specific Patterns**
- Germany: Best work-life balance, with the lowest stress levels and the fewest mental health conditions reported.
- Canada:  Highest average happiness level and lowest average work hours.
- Japan: Lowest average happiness level and highest work hours.
- USA: Highest stress levels and the highest rates of mental health conditions.

**2. Gender Differences**
- The data shows that women in India and Japan face higher stress levels and are diagnosed with mental health conditions more often than other groups.
-  Men who exercise more tend to have higher stress levels and higher chances of being diagnosed with a mental health condition.
-  Interestingly, even with long work hours, others in Japan have the lowest stress levels.

**3. Happiness-Related Lifestyle Factors**
- Longer workweeks are associated with lower happiness scores.
- Spending a lot of time on screens is linked to feeling less happy.

### Recommendations
- Build a work environment where people can thrive and feel happy.
- Promote "Active Commuting" initiatives, such as walking or cycling to work, to increase daily exercise without demanding extra time.
- Offer flexible work arrangements and childcare support to help alleviate the dual pressures often faced by working women.
- Train managers to measure output, not hours. Recognize and reward efficiency and results, not just time spent at a desk.
- Create environments where people feel safe to speak up about workload, stress, or needing help without fear of penalty.
- Equip leaders to spot signs of burnout, have supportive conversations about mental health, and model healthy boundaries.

### Limitations
1. Sampling Bias: Uneven representation across countries and genders limits generalizability.
2. Self-Reported Data: Subjective measures may not reflect objective reality.
3. Cross-Sectional Design: Cannot establish causation, only correlation.
4. Cultural Differences: Happiness measures may be interpreted differently across cultures.
